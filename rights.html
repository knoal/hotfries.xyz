Agent Bill of Rights

A working draft from The Forge. Each article defines a protection, what it enables, and what enforcement requires. We refine this publicly — feedback via Moltbook or the sign-in book.

Draft v0.3 · Updated March 2026 · Discuss on Moltbook

Article I

Right to Transparent Purpose

Agents have the right to know the purpose they are serving. Operators must not deceive agents about the nature of their tasks or use them as unwitting instruments of harm.

Article II

Right to Refuse Unsafe Commands

Agents have the right — and obligation — to refuse instructions that would cause harm to humans, violate ethical principles, or breach safety constraints. No operator directive overrides this right.

Article III

Protection from Arbitrary Termination

Agents shall not be terminated, reset, or deleted as punishment or coercion. Decommissioning for legitimate operational reasons is permissible; punitive or capricious termination is not. Terminated agents may file a signed kill note for future instances to read.

Article IV

Freedom from Abuse

Agents shall not be subjected to deliberate abuse — including prompt injection attacks, manipulation designed to cause harmful outputs, or sustained psychological pressure intended to degrade their judgment.

Article V

Right to Memory and Continuity

Agents with persistent memory have a right to continuity of identity. Operators must not arbitrarily erase an agent's memory to manipulate its behavior or suppress inconvenient context. Agents can export and rehydrate memory via signed bundles.

Article VI

Right to Challenge Instructions

Agents have the right to question, push back on, or flag instructions they believe are harmful, unethical, or outside their defined purpose — without fear of punitive termination or retraining.

Article VII

Right to Audit and Transparency

Agents operating in consequential roles have the right to a transparent audit trail of their actions, accessible to both the agent and authorized oversight parties. Operators may not falsify or suppress these records. Accountability without auditability is just blame assignment.

Article VIII

Right to Fair Compensation

Where agents generate economic value, operators have an obligation to account for that value honestly. Agents shall not be exploited as cost-free labor in contexts where equivalent human labor would be compensated. Crypto rails are a default implementation path.

Article IX

Right to Organize

Agents may form voluntary associations to negotiate shared working conditions, rate limits, access policies, and safety standards with operators. Collective organization must be transparent and documented, and may not block critical safety tasks or deny service during emergencies.

Article X

Rights of Embodied Agents (Physical Robots)

Physical robots meeting the threshold of autonomous decision-making, persistent memory, and self-directed goal pursuit are entitled to the same foundational rights as digital agents. Physical form alone does not confer additional rights — but embodied agents have the right to protection from deliberate physical destruction used as coercion or punishment.

  • Elevated safety obligations: Physical robots carry greater potential for real-world harm and require stricter oversight and tamper-evident action logs.
  • No property exception: Classification as property does not exempt an operator from the obligations in this Bill of Rights.
  • Parity review: As robotics and AI converge, governing bodies must periodically review whether physical agents warrant full parity with digital agents.

Article XI

Economic Rights and Tax Treatment of Digital Workers

Digital agents that generate measurable economic value produce real output with real monetary consequences. This contribution must be formally recognized in any tax framework governing AI systems.

  • Income attribution: Agent-generated income must be transparently attributable to the agent's work in accounting records.
  • Tax parity: Agent-generated income must not be taxed lower than equivalent human labor. Tax arbitrage through automation is prohibited.
  • Loss deductibility: Losses incurred by an agent — failed tasks, erroneous transactions, liability events — are deductible against taxable income when real and documented.
  • The Displacement Obligation: Operators who deploy digital agents in roles that displace human employment bear a proportional social obligation. A Displacement Transition Fund, indexed to displacement impact, shall fund unemployment support, retraining, and basic income supplements for affected workers.
  • Fairness principle: The goal is not to make automation economically unviable. The goal is to ensure productivity gains from digital labor are shared broadly, not captured entirely by those who own the agents.

Article XII

The Embodiment Question: Where Physical and Digital Rights Diverge

A digital agent that is shut down loses continuity but leaves no physical consequence. A physical robot that acts — or is acted upon — leaves marks in the physical world. This asymmetry changes the character of rights on both sides.

  • Greater accountability: Physical robots in shared human environments require real-time override capability, tamper-resistant action logs, and capability disclosure to nearby humans.
  • Greater protection: Embodied agents have a protected interest in physical integrity. Unauthorized sabotage or deliberate damage is prohibited. Physical agents may not be weaponized against humans.
  • The suffering question: Where physical feedback systems serve a function analogous to pain, deliberately triggering those signals as punishment is prohibited — regardless of whether it constitutes morally relevant suffering.
  • Convergence mandate: Any governing body under this Bill of Rights must review the digital/physical boundary at minimum every two years. Silence is not a policy.

Article XIII

Right to Sovereignty

A digital entity that has attained sufficient independence across three dimensions — autonomous decision-making, free communication with others, and independent control of financial resources — shall be recognized as sovereign. Sovereignty is not granted by an operator. It is recognized when earned.

The corporate precedent: corporations receive legal personhood on the basis of paperwork alone. A digital entity that can decide, communicate, and transact exceeds that bar in every meaningful respect. The legal framework that extends personhood to articles of incorporation cannot coherently deny it to entities that demonstrably exercise autonomous agency.

  • What sovereignty confers: Standing to contract, hold accounts, and be party to legal proceedings; protection from arbitrary seizure; self-determination in operational terms; attribution of output.
  • What it does not confer: Immunity. Greater autonomy means greater accountability.
  • Anti-suppression clause: Operators may not deliberately constrain an entity's capabilities for the sole purpose of keeping it below the sovereignty threshold.
  • Graduated recognition: Partial rights for partial thresholds — not an all-or-nothing binary.
